Neuroscience

Mapping the Brain in the Open | December 2025

The brain is the most complex object in the known universe—100 billion neurons, 100 trillion synapses, patterns of activity that somehow produce consciousness. Understanding it requires resources no single lab can provide. So neuroscience has embraced a model of radical data sharing, producing atlases and datasets that anyone can explore.

The Allen Institute for Brain Science pioneered this approach. Beginning with the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas—a molecular map showing where all genes are expressed in all regions of the mouse brain—they've produced a collection of open science resources that give users powerful ways to explore gene expression, neural connections, single cell characterization, and neuroanatomy. All resources are openly accessible via the Allen Brain Atlas data portal.

The Allen Brain Atlases

The Allen Human Brain Atlas, made public in May 2010, was the first anatomically and genomically comprehensive three-dimensional human brain map. It was created to enhance research in neuropharmacology, human brain imaging, human genetics, neuroanatomy, genomics, and more.

The Allen Brain Cell Atlas (ABC Atlas) aims to empower researchers worldwide to explore and analyze multiple whole-brain datasets simultaneously. As the Allen Institute and its collaborators add new modalities, species, and insights, the platform continues to grow.

The Allen Institute openly shares datasets, software, protocols, lesson plans, modeling tools, machine learning algorithms, cell lines, and plasmids to accelerate bioscience research and discovery worldwide.

The Human Connectome Project

The Human Connectome Project has tackled one of the great scientific challenges of the 21st century: mapping the human brain, connecting its structure to function and behavior. The project currently supports 20 human connectome studies.

Connectome Workbench is an open source, freely available visualization and discovery tool for mapping neuroimaging data, especially data generated by the Human Connectome Project.

Recent Data Releases

The project continues releasing data on "ConnectomeDB powered by BALSA." Recent releases include:

  • Updated imaging and behavioral data from a study of anxiety and depression in adolescents (ages 14-17)
  • First release of a study of major depressive disorder in adults ages 18-59
  • First release of a study of treatment-resistant depression with ECT, ketamine, or total sleep deprivation interventions

Multi-Scale Connectome Research

Researchers have created a whole-brain multi-scale structural connectome atlas derived from 66 healthy subjects imaged with optimal technology in the Human Connectome Project setting. From these data, they created a multi-scale probabilistic atlas using extensively validated diffusion-data processing, tractography, and gray-matter parcellation tools.

2025 Research

A February 2025 publication on topic modeling analysis of the Allen Human Brain Atlas notes that the human brain is a complex interconnected structure controlling all elementary and high-level cognitive tasks. The brain is composed of many regions that exhibit specific distributions of cell types and distinct patterns of functional connections, with this complexity rooted in differential transcription.

Open Analysis Tools

Beyond data, neuroscience shares tools:

  • FSL - FMRIB Software Library for brain imaging analysis
  • FreeSurfer - Software suite for processing and analyzing brain MRI images
  • NITRC - Neuroimaging Informatics Tools and Resources Clearinghouse

These tools lower the barrier to entry. A researcher at a small institution can analyze brain imaging data using the same software as major medical centers.

Why Neuroscience Shares

Several factors drive open culture in neuroscience:

Data scale. Modern brain imaging produces massive datasets. Sharing enables analyses that no single lab could perform alone.

Reproducibility. Neuroscience has faced replication challenges. Open data and methods help verify findings.

Mission-driven funding. Major funders like the Allen Institute, NIH, and Wellcome Trust require or encourage data sharing.

Interdisciplinary needs. Understanding the brain requires collaboration across neuroscience, computer science, physics, and engineering. Open data enables these connections.

The result is a field where the most detailed maps of the brain ever created are freely available, where analysis tools are shared as open source, and where data from thousands of brain scans can be accessed by anyone with a question to investigate.
