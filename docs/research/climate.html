Climate Science

The 1.5°C Threshold | December 2025

In January 2025, the World Meteorological Organization confirmed what independent analyses from NASA, NOAA, Berkeley Earth, and Copernicus had all concluded: 2024 was the hottest year in human history—and the first to exceed 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels. The last 10 years are the 10 hottest on record, likely the hottest in 125,000 years.

Climate science operates on a simple premise: the data belongs to everyone. When the stakes involve the entire planet's future, restricting access serves no one. The major agencies—NASA, NOAA, ESA—publish openly. The IPCC synthesizes findings into freely available reports. Climate models run on open-source code anyone can inspect.

The Temperature Record

2024: Breaking Records

NASA confirmed 2024 as the warmest year since records began in 1850, with temperatures 1.29°C (2.32°F) above the 20th-century average and 1.55°C above pre-industrial levels. For more than half the year, average temperatures exceeded 1.5°C above baseline.

Multiple independent groups reached the same conclusion using different methodologies: NOAA, Berkeley Earth, the UK Met Office's Hadley Centre, and Copernicus. "The primary reason for these record temperatures is the accumulation of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere" from burning fossil fuels, noted Copernicus strategic climate lead Samantha Burgess.

2025: Continuing the Trend

Carbon Brief reports 2025 is tracking as the second or third warmest year on record. Through November, global surface temperature was only 0.01°C below the same period in record-setting 2024. November 2025 ranked as the third-warmest November in NOAA's 176-year record.

Remarkably, the first six months of 2025 were extremely warm despite moderate La Niña conditions, which typically suppress global temperatures. The WMO expects the 2023-2025 three-year average will exceed 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels for the first time.

The Paris Agreement Context

Exceeding 1.5°C for a single year doesn't breach the Paris Agreement threshold, which refers to a 30-year average. But the symbolic and practical significance is clear. Scientific American notes that overshooting 1.5°C means more extreme weather, accelerated sea level rise, and ecosystem stress.

The long-term warming rate has accelerated. According to NOAA's 2024 report, combined land-ocean temperature has warmed 0.11°F per decade since 1850—but more than three times that rate (0.36°F per decade) since 1975.

IPCC Seventh Assessment

The IPCC is preparing its Seventh Assessment Report (AR7). During its February 2025 plenary session in Hangzhou, the panel agreed on outlines for the three Working Group contributions. At the October 2025 session in Lima, they finalized content for the 2027 Methodology Report on Carbon Dioxide Removal Technologies. The Synthesis Report is expected by late 2029.

The Emissions Gap

The UNEP Emissions Gap Report 2025 delivers sobering findings. Even with full implementation of current Nationally Determined Contributions, temperatures could rise 2.3-2.5°C this century. Based on current policies alone: 2.8°C. The U.S. withdrawal from the Paris Agreement will cancel about 0.1°C of projected improvement.

The State of Climate Action 2025 from the World Resources Institute is blunt: "Not one of the 45 indicators assessed is on track to achieve its 2030 target." Global efforts to reduce emissions are failing at the pace and scale needed.

Why Climate Science Must Be Open

Climate doesn't respect borders. A temperature record from Antarctica matters to researchers in Norway. Satellite data from ESA informs models at NOAA. International collaboration requires international data sharing.

Public funding. Most climate research is government-funded. Taxpayers paid for it; taxpayers can access it.

Policy relevance. Climate data informs decisions affecting billions. Hiding it behind paywalls would undermine public trust and democratic deliberation.

Verification needs. Climate science faces organized denial. Open data and methods allow anyone to check the work—and confirms the science holds up.

The result is the most openly accessible body of science affecting public policy. Anyone can download the same temperature records that informed IPCC reports. Anyone can trace how we know 2024 was the hottest year ever recorded. The science that tells us we've crossed 1.5°C—temporarily, for now—is available to all.
