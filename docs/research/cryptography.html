Cryptography & Security

The Post-Quantum Transition Begins | December 2025

On August 13, 2024, NIST released the first three post-quantum cryptography standards—ready for immediate use. On March 11, 2025, they selected HQC as a backup algorithm. Between these announcements, Chrome switched to ML-KEM, Apple announced quantum-resistant iMessage, and Signal became the first major messaging app with post-quantum key exchange. The quantum-safe transition has moved from theory to deployment.

The International Association for Cryptologic Research maintains the Cryptology ePrint Archive, where these algorithms faced years of public scrutiny before standardization. Security through obscurity doesn't work. Only schemes that survive sustained attack from the research community deserve trust.

The NIST Standards

The August 2024 release finalized three standards after an eight-year process that began with a 2016 call for proposals:

FIPS 203: ML-KEM

ML-KEM (Module-Lattice-Based Key-Encapsulation Mechanism), based on CRYSTALS-Kyber, is the primary standard for key exchange. Its security relies on the Module Learning with Errors problem, believed to resist both classical and quantum attacks. The standard specifies three parameter sets—ML-KEM-512, ML-KEM-768, and ML-KEM-1024—in order of increasing security and decreasing performance.

FIPS 204 and 205: Digital Signatures

FIPS 204 (ML-DSA) provides lattice-based digital signatures. FIPS 205 (SLH-DSA) offers hash-based signatures as a conservative alternative. Together with the earlier SP 800-208 (LMS/XMSS), organizations now have multiple quantum-resistant signature options.

HQC: The Backup

On March 11, 2025, NIST selected HQC (Hamming Quasi-Cyclic) for standardization as a backup to ML-KEM. "We want a backup standard based on a different math approach than ML-KEM," NIST explained. "It's essential to have a fallback in case ML-KEM proves vulnerable." HQC uses code-based cryptography rather than lattices. The final standard is expected in 2027 after a 90-day comment period.

Real-World Deployment

Chrome and the Web

Chrome 131 (November 2024) switched from experimental Kyber to finalized ML-KEM for post-quantum key exchange. This wasn't a minor update—the two versions are incompatible, requiring a clean transition. Cloudflare reports working with Chrome to test Merkle tree-based post-quantum authentication by end of 2025.

Signal: First to Production

Signal implemented PQXDH, becoming the first large-scale messaging app with post-quantum security in initial key establishment. Signal chats are now secured with post-quantum cryptography.

Apple: Going Further

Apple's PQ3 protocol for iMessage goes beyond Signal's approach. While Signal uses post-quantum algorithms only during initialization, PQ3 incorporates them into the ongoing ratcheting process, using quantum-resistant algorithms repeatedly throughout the conversation. During WWDC25, Apple announced iOS 26, macOS Tahoe 26, and visionOS 26 will support quantum-secure TLS 1.3 key exchange.

Cloud Infrastructure

AWS provides ML-KEM through AWS-LC, their FIPS-validated cryptographic library. Cryptographic service endpoints support hybrid ML-KEM cipher suites, enabling organizations to migrate while maintaining backward compatibility.

Migration Timelines

Governments worldwide have set PQC migration deadlines:

  • United States: Full migration by 2035; NSA mandated quantum-resistant algorithms for certain applications as early as 2025
  • Australia: Aggressive 2030 deadline set in 2024
  • United Kingdom: Matched US 2035 timeline in early 2025
  • European Union: 2030-2035 depending on application category

The urgency comes from "harvest now, decrypt later" attacks—adversaries collecting encrypted traffic today, betting future quantum computers will break current encryption. Data with long-term sensitivity (medical records, classified documents, financial data) is already at risk.

Hybrid Cryptography

NIST permits hybrid key exchange—combining ML-KEM with classical algorithms like X25519. This provides defense in depth: if either algorithm is broken, the other still protects the exchange. European authorities particularly recommend hybrid adoption as a practical interim step.

Hybrid signatures remain more complex. NIST doesn't yet support them officially, though some implementations combine classical and post-quantum signatures for added assurance.

Why Cryptography Opens Its Research

Cryptography presents a paradox: a field devoted to secrets that publishes openly. The resolution is simple—only algorithms that survive sustained attack deserve trust. Papers appear on ePrint without peer review; the community provides scrutiny. A flawed scheme attracts attacks within days.

This matters especially now. The algorithms protecting internet traffic for the next decades are being finalized. ML-KEM, HQC, and the signature schemes must be examined by every capable cryptographer. The open process that produced these standards—eight years of public competition, thousands of researcher-hours of analysis—is why we can trust them with our long-term secrets.
