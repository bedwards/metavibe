Pushing the Boundaries

The Multiplanetary Species

When Humanity Establishes Permanent Presence Beyond Earth | December 2025

SpaceX launched 170 times in 2025—more than the entire rest of the world combined. Starship demonstrated successful booster recovery and orbital insertion. Artemis III targets 2027 for the first crewed lunar landing since 1972. China aims for its own Moon landing by 2030. The technology for permanent off-world presence is materializing faster than the institutions to govern it.

The Starship Inflection Point

Starship's 100-150 metric ton payload capacity to low Earth orbit, fully reusable, changes the economics of everything beyond. Previous rockets cost $2,000-20,000 per kilogram to orbit. Starship targets under $100—potentially under $10 with full reusability.

At those economics, space stations become space cities. Moon bases become permanent settlements. Mars missions become routine rather than heroic. The constraint shifts from "can we afford to launch it" to "can we build it fast enough."

What if launching to orbit cost less than transcontinental shipping?

Step One: Lunar Infrastructure

Artemis establishes the first sustained human presence on the Moon. Gateway provides an orbital station. Starship HLS lands crew on the surface. But Artemis is still an expedition—astronauts visit and return.

The next step: infrastructure that stays. In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) extracts water from lunar ice at the poles. Electrolysis splits water into hydrogen and oxygen—rocket propellant. The Moon becomes a refueling station, not just a destination.

Solar panels deployed on crater rims receive near-constant sunlight. Nuclear reactors (NASA's Kilopower) provide baseline power. Regolith shields habitats from radiation. Each component exists in prototype. Integration is the remaining work.

Step Two: The Cislunar Economy

With lunar propellant production, missions beyond the Moon become dramatically cheaper. A spacecraft refueled on the Moon doesn't need to haul Earth-escape propellant from Earth's deep gravity well. Mars missions originate from lunar orbit.

What's worth doing in cislunar space? Scientific instruments on the far side—radio telescopes shielded from Earth's noise. Orbital manufacturing in microgravity—fiber optics, pharmaceuticals. Asteroid mining operations staged from lunar bases. Tourism, eventually.

The economics are speculative but the physics is proven. Every step reduces cost for the next. The question is whether investment sustains long enough for returns to compound.

Step Three: Mars Transit

SpaceX's stated goal remains Mars colonization. Starship's methane/oxygen propulsion was chosen because both can be synthesized on Mars from atmospheric CO2 and subsurface water ice. The architecture assumes ISRU from day one.

Transit time: 6-9 months depending on orbital alignment. Optimal launch windows occur every 26 months. First crewed missions require prepositioned supplies—cargo Starships landing before humans arrive. Life support, power systems, habitats, all waiting.

Radiation exposure during transit remains a challenge. Solar events can deliver lethal doses in hours. Shielding adds mass. Active systems (magnetic deflection) remain experimental. The current approach: accept elevated cancer risk for volunteers, screen for radiation resistance.

Step Four: Mars Settlement

A self-sustaining Mars colony requires more than survival—it requires reproduction and industrial capacity. The population needed for genetic diversity: estimates range from 500 to 50,000 depending on assumptions. The industrial base needed for self-sufficiency: unknown, but certainly larger than any near-term mission.

Iterate forward: early missions establish ISRU capability. Subsequent missions expand. Each launch window delivers more equipment, more people. After a decade, a permanent base. After a generation, a town. After a century, independence from Earth supply lines becomes conceivable.

This is Elon Musk's stated vision. Whether SpaceX maintains focus, funding, and capability over the required timescales is the largest uncertainty.

Perspective: The Astronaut

For early Mars settlers, the decision is one-way—not because return is impossible, but because the mission duration exceeds any reasonable employment contract. You don't commute to Mars. You emigrate.

The psychological challenges of permanent settlement differ from exploration missions. On the ISS, home is visible overhead. On Mars, Earth is a bright star. The isolation is qualitatively different. Selection criteria shift from technical competence alone to community-building capacity.

Perspective: The Nation

Space law, established in the 1967 Outer Space Treaty, was designed for a world of competing superpowers and minimal commercial activity. It declares celestial bodies the "province of all mankind" while saying little about resource extraction or permanent settlement.

The 2020 Artemis Accords attempted to establish norms for lunar activity but remain voluntary and disputed. China and Russia are not signatories. If multiple nations establish permanent bases, under whose law do settlers live? The question is not hypothetical—2030 approaches.

Sovereignty extends to permanent settlements. A Mars colony dependent on SpaceX launches is effectively a SpaceX territory, regardless of the nationalities of its inhabitants. The power dynamics of space settlement may not resemble nation-states at all.

Perspective: The Species

The multiplanetary argument reduces to existential risk: a single-planet species can be extinguished by a single event. Asteroid impact, supervolcanic eruption, engineered pandemic, nuclear war—low-probability events that become near-certainties over geological time.

A second population on another planet provides backup. Even a small Mars colony—100 people with the knowledge to rebuild—changes the expected lifespan of human civilization from "until the next extinction event" to "potentially indefinite."

This framing is controversial. The resources for Mars colonization could address Earth problems. The risks of settlement are borne by colonists while benefits (species survival) accrue to humanity abstractly. The ethics of existential risk are underdeveloped.

The Timeline

Conservative estimate: First crewed Mars landing, 2030s. First permanent base, 2040s. First Mars-born human, 2050s. Self-sustaining colony, 22nd century.

Aggressive estimate (SpaceX targets): Crewed landing by 2030. City-scale settlement by 2050. Million-person colony by 2100.

The gap between these timelines is enormous—decades versus generations. Which materializes depends on factors ranging from rocket reliability to economic sustainability to political will. The technology is converging. The institutions lag. The boundaries we're pushing are as much organizational as physical.
